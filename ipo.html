The giving journey

Four questions that shape your plan

A little planning can go a very long way. Getting clear on these four areas will help you give more to the causes you care about — at a lower effective cost.

What can I give?

Take stock of your full financial picture — equity types (RSUs, NSOs, ISOs), vesting schedules, and other appreciated assets beyond your IPO compensation.

When should I give?

Timing donations in high-income years maximizes tax deductions. Understand lockup periods, tax year deadlines, and when your gains are realized.

How much should I give?

Balance your personal financial needs with the unique opportunity of a windfall. Consider what you need for a good life, and what your optimal giving level is from a tax perspective.

How should I give?

Direct gifts, Donor Advised Funds, and other vehicles each have distinct advantages. The right choice depends on your timeline and how much flexibility you need.

Step 01

Know what you hold

Before making any giving decisions, take an exhaustive look at your income and assets. Understand the types of equity you hold — RSUs, NSOs, ISOs — along with grant dates, vesting schedules, and current fair market value. Your stock grant documents or cap table manager will have this information.

It is generally beneficial to make donations in high-income years. Donating highly appreciated securities can be especially advantageous: you avoid capital gains tax on the gains and receive an income tax deduction for the full fair market value. That means a bigger donation, a higher deduction, and greater overall tax savings.

Key assets to review

  • RSUs (Restricted Stock Units) — vested and unvested
  • NSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options)
  • ISOs (Incentive Stock Options)
  • ESPP shares held longer than one year
  • Other appreciated assets beyond IPO equity

Why appreciated stock?

When you donate long-term appreciated stock directly to a qualified charity, you skip capital gains tax entirely and claim a deduction at full market value. This makes your gift go further at a lower cost to you.

Step 02

Time it right

Companies typically go public two to three months after an S-1 filing, followed by a lockup period (usually around six months) during which employees cannot trade shares. Once the lockup expires, you can begin selling — and donating.

The critical principle: donate in the year you realize your gain to offset the tax deduction against your highest-income period. Be mindful that the tax year ends December 31, and the holiday season is an exceptionally busy time for DAFs and charities. Plan ahead to avoid the rush and ensure your gifts are processed in time.

Example: the power of appreciated stock

Stock basis (what you paid) $10,000
Current market value $110,000
Capital gains avoided (25% rate) $25,000
Income tax deduction (40% rate) $44,000
Effective cost of a $110K gift $41,000

Illustrative example. Consult a tax advisor for your specific situation.

Step 03

Decide what feels right

A windfall from an IPO can be a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity to be exceptionally generous. This is compensation above and beyond what you've become used to — and an excellent time to give more than you would in an ordinary year.

The decision comes down to two angles: how much do you need to live a good life, and what is your optimal giving level from a tax perspective? In the best case, your final decision takes both into account. It may help to know that Founders Pledge members who've joined in recent years have generously pledged to give an average of 12% of their exit proceeds to charity.

Two questions to consider

What do I need for a good life?

Plan and anticipate your financial needs. Prepare to give what remains beyond that.

What's my optimal giving level?

Work with a financial advisor to understand the tax-advantaged ceiling for your donations.

Step 04

Choose the right vehicle

Once you know how much to give, decide whether to donate directly to charities or use a giving vehicle like a Donor Advised Fund (DAF). A DAF is a charitable savings account — you transfer assets, claim the tax deduction immediately, and take your time deciding which organizations to support.

This is especially useful if the tax year is ending and you haven't yet chosen your charities. You can also invest assets inside a DAF, growing your charitable pot over time. Keep in mind that most DAFs have minimum requirements and charge fees — Founders Pledge operates a zero-fee global DAF for its members.

Commonly asked questions

In most cases, you'll need to wait until the lockup period expires to donate public shares. However, some companies allow pre-IPO donations of private shares to qualified charities or DAFs. Check with your company's equity administration team and a tax advisor for specifics.

A DAF is simpler and less expensive to operate. You get an immediate tax deduction, can invest assets, and make grants to charities over time — all without the legal overhead, required annual distributions, or excise taxes associated with a private foundation. For most individuals facing an IPO, a DAF is the more practical option.

The most tax-advantageous assets to donate are typically long-term appreciated securities — stock you've held for over one year with significant unrealized gains. By donating these instead of selling and giving cash, you avoid capital gains tax and can claim a deduction at the full fair market value. A financial advisor can help you identify which specific holdings to prioritize.

Yes. Evidence Action can receive donations of publicly traded stock as well as grants from Donor Advised Funds. Contact donate@evidenceaction.org for transfer details and to coordinate gifts of appreciated securities.

Charitable deductions for gifts of appreciated stock to public charities are generally limited to 30% of your adjusted gross income (AGI). Cash gifts can be deducted up to 60% of AGI. Unused deductions can typically be carried forward for up to five years. Your specific limits depend on the type of charity and the asset — a tax advisor can help you navigate the details.
